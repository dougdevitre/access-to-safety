## Overview

Technology is increasingly used as a tool of abuse, surveillance, and control. Abusers use shared accounts, tracking devices, spyware, social media monitoring, and location-sharing features to maintain power over survivors — often even after the survivor has physically left. Digital safety is not separate from physical safety; a compromised device can reveal a survivor's location, safety plan, or communications with advocates.

This pod covers how to identify tech-enabled abuse, secure devices and accounts, respond to specific threats (stalkerware, AirTags, doxxing, sextortion, NCII), and report online exploitation including CSAM.

## Key Concepts and Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Stalkerware / Spyware** | Software secretly installed on a device that allows another person to monitor calls, texts, location, browsing, and keystrokes. Often marketed as "parental monitoring" tools. |
| **AirTag / Bluetooth tracker abuse** | Using Apple AirTags, Tile, Samsung SmartTags, or similar devices to secretly track a person's location by hiding the tracker in their belongings. |
| **Doxxing** | Publishing a person's private information (home address, phone number, workplace) online without their consent, often to facilitate harassment or stalking. |
| **NCII (Non-Consensual Intimate Images)** | Sharing or threatening to share intimate/sexual images without the subject's consent. Sometimes called "revenge porn." This is a crime in Missouri. |
| **Sextortion** | Threatening to share intimate images or information unless the victim provides money, more images, or other demands. |
| **CSAM (Child Sexual Abuse Material)** | Any visual depiction of sexually explicit conduct involving a minor. Possession, distribution, and production are federal crimes. Report to NCMEC. |
| **Cyberstalking** | Using electronic communication to stalk, harass, or threaten. A federal crime under 18 U.S.C. § 2261A and a Missouri crime under RSMo § 565.225. |
| **Digital coercive control** | Using technology as part of a broader pattern of control — monitoring messages, demanding passwords, controlling access to devices, using GPS to track movements. |
| **IP logging** | Capturing a person's IP address through links or images to determine their approximate location. |
| **Account compromise** | When an abuser has access to a survivor's email, cloud storage, or social media — either through shared passwords, password recovery access, or device access. |

## Recognizing Tech-Enabled Abuse

**Signs your device may be compromised:**
- Battery drains faster than normal
- Phone runs hot when not in use
- Unusual data usage spikes
- Abuser knows details of conversations or locations they should not know
- Unfamiliar apps appear on your device
- Your phone makes sounds or lights up when you are not using it
- Settings change without your action (Bluetooth turns on, location services activate)

**Signs of location tracking:**
- Abuser shows up at places they should not know you are
- Abuser references your movements ("I know you went to...")
- You find unfamiliar objects in your car, bag, or jacket (AirTags, Tiles)
- Your phone alerts you that an unknown AirTag is traveling with you (iPhone) or you see an "Unknown tracker alert" (Android)

**Signs of account compromise:**
- Password changes you did not make
- Login alerts from devices or locations you do not recognize
- Emails or messages marked as read that you have not opened
- Friends receive messages from your account that you did not send
- Your email recovery phone or address has been changed

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Securing Your Phone

> **Important safety note:** If your abuser monitors your device and suddenly notices changes (new passwords, removed apps), this can escalate danger. Consider making changes from a safe device first, or consult an advocate before changing anything.

**If you need a safe device immediately:**
- Use a public library computer (clear history when done)
- Ask a trusted friend to borrow their phone
- Contact a local DV organization — many provide safe phones
- National DV Hotline: 1-800-799-7233 (ask about safe phone programs)

**Securing your current phone:**

1. **Check for stalkerware:**
   - iPhone: Stalkerware is rare on iPhones that have not been jailbroken. Check Settings > Privacy & Security > Location Services for unfamiliar apps with location access. Check Settings > General > VPN & Device Management for unknown profiles.
   - Android: Go to Settings > Apps and look for unfamiliar apps, especially those with accessibility or admin permissions. Check Settings > Security > Device admin apps for unknown entries.
   - Use the free **Coalition Against Stalkerware** resources at stopstalkerware.org for detection guidance.

2. **Check for AirTags / Bluetooth trackers:**
   - iPhone: You will receive an automatic alert if an unknown AirTag is traveling with you.
   - Android: Download the **Tracker Detect** app (by Apple) or enable Google's built-in unknown tracker alerts.
   - Physically check: Look in your car (wheel wells, under seats, in trunk), bags, jacket pockets, and belongings.
   - If you find one: Do NOT destroy it (it is evidence). Note the serial number, take a photo, and report it to police.

3. **Change passwords (from a safe device):**
   - Start with your primary email — this is the key to all other accounts
   - Use a new, strong password your abuser cannot guess (not a birthday, pet name, or variation of an old password)
   - Change: email, banking, social media, cloud storage (iCloud, Google), phone carrier account
   - Use a password manager (Bitwarden is free and secure)

4. **Enable two-factor authentication (2FA):**
   - Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy) rather than text messages — text-based 2FA can be intercepted if the abuser has access to your phone account
   - Enable 2FA on: email, banking, social media, cloud storage

5. **Review shared access:**
   - Location sharing: Disable Find My Friends, Google Maps location sharing, Life360
   - Shared accounts: Remove your abuser from shared iCloud, Google, Amazon, streaming accounts
   - Linked devices: In each account's security settings, review and remove devices you do not recognize
   - Phone carrier: Call your carrier and set a PIN on your account to prevent unauthorized changes

### Procedure 2: Responding to NCII (Non-Consensual Intimate Images)

1. **You are not at fault.** Sharing intimate images without consent is a crime, regardless of how the images were originally created.
2. **Do not engage with the person threatening you.** Do not pay, do not send more images, do not negotiate.
3. **Preserve evidence:**
   - Screenshot the threats (include the sender's username/number and timestamps)
   - Save the URL if images are posted online
   - Do not delete any messages
4. **Report and request removal:**
   - **StopNCII.org** — A free tool by the Revenge Porn Helpline that creates a hash of your images and works with platforms (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Reddit) to block them from being uploaded
   - **Platform reporting:** Use the platform's reporting tools to flag the content as NCII
   - **Google:** Request removal from search results at support.google.com/websearch (search "remove non-consensual explicit images")
5. **Report to law enforcement:**
   - Missouri law: RSMo § 573.110 criminalizes non-consensual dissemination of private sexual images. It is a class D felony.
   - File a police report with your local department
   - If the images involve a minor: this is CSAM — report to NCMEC (see Procedure 4)
6. **Get support:**
   - Cyber Civil Rights Initiative: cybercivilrights.org — crisis helpline 844-878-2274
   - National DV Hotline: 1-800-799-7233

### Procedure 3: Responding to Doxxing

1. **Document everything** before it is removed: screenshot the post, note the platform, URL, date, and username.
2. **Report to the platform** where your information was posted. Most major platforms prohibit doxxing.
3. **Google yourself** to see what information is publicly available. Request removal of personal info:
   - Google: Use the "Results about you" tool in Google account settings
   - Data broker opt-outs: Sites like Spokeo, WhitePages, BeenVerified allow you to request removal
4. **Secure your accounts** (see Procedure 1 — password changes and 2FA).
5. **Alert your workplace and school** if your address or workplace was posted.
6. **Contact local police** if the doxxing includes threats of violence or is part of stalking.
7. **Consider a P.O. Box or virtual mailbox** for future mail to keep your physical address private.

### Procedure 4: Reporting CSAM (Child Sexual Abuse Material)

**If you encounter CSAM online:**
1. **Do NOT download, screenshot, or share the material.** Possession is a federal crime, even if your intent is to report it.
2. **Report immediately to NCMEC:** CyberTipline.org or call **1-800-843-5678** (1-800-THE-LOST)
3. **Report to the platform** where you found it.
4. **Report to the FBI:** tips.fbi.gov
5. **If a child is in immediate danger:** Call **911**.

**If a child tells you they are being exploited online:**
1. Believe them. Stay calm.
2. Do not ask to see the images or material.
3. Report to NCMEC CyberTipline (above) and local police.
4. In Missouri, this triggers mandatory reporting obligations — call **1-800-392-3738**.
5. Connect the child to support: Childhelp 1-800-422-4453.

### Procedure 5: Responding to Sextortion

1. **Do not pay.** Paying does not stop the threats — it usually leads to more demands.
2. **Do not send additional images.**
3. **Stop all communication** with the person if safe to do so.
4. **Preserve evidence:** Screenshot all messages, demands, and threats.
5. **Report:**
   - FBI: tips.fbi.gov or local FBI field office (St. Louis: 314-589-2500)
   - If the victim is under 18: NCMEC CyberTipline (CyberTipline.org)
   - Local police
6. **Get support:**
   - For minors: Thorn's sextortion resources at thorn.org
   - Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
   - National DV Hotline: 1-800-799-7233

## Missouri Legal Framework

| Statute / Law | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **RSMo § 573.110** | Non-consensual dissemination of private sexual images — class D felony |
| **RSMo § 565.225** | Cyberstalking — using electronic communication to harass, intimidate, or threaten. Class D felony for aggravated (threats of death/injury). |
| **RSMo § 565.227** | Harassment in the first degree — electronic harassment targeting a specific person |
| **RSMo § 573.025–573.037** | Child sexual exploitation and CSAM — state-level offenses |
| **18 U.S.C. § 2261A** | Federal cyberstalking statute |
| **18 U.S.C. § 2252** | Federal CSAM possession and distribution |
| **47 U.S.C. § 230(e)(1)** | Section 230 does NOT protect platforms from federal criminal law related to CSAM |
