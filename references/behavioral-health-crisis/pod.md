## Overview

Behavioral health crises include mental health emergencies (suicidal ideation, psychosis, severe anxiety or panic, self-harm), substance use crises (overdose, withdrawal, relapse), and co-occurring situations where both mental health and substance use are involved. In St. Louis County, these crises are addressed through the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, BHR mobile crisis teams, hospital psychiatric emergency services, crisis stabilization units, and substance use treatment providers.

This pod covers the full continuum from acute crisis through stabilization and early recovery connection. For the initial dispatch and triage process, see the **Crisis Response** pod. For crises related to violence or abuse, see the **Violence & Abuse** pod.

## Key Concepts and Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline** | National crisis line (call or text 988). Routes to local crisis centers. Replaced the old 10-digit number in July 2022. |
| **Suicidal ideation** | Thoughts about ending one's life, ranging from passive ("I wish I weren't here") to active ("I have a plan to..."). All levels should be taken seriously. |
| **96-hour hold (involuntary detention)** | Under Missouri RSMo § 632.305, a person may be held up to 96 hours for psychiatric evaluation if they pose an imminent danger to themselves or others due to a mental disorder. |
| **Voluntary admission** | Under RSMo § 632.300, a person can voluntarily admit themselves to a psychiatric facility. They may request discharge, which the facility must act on within 3 days unless involuntary proceedings are initiated. |
| **Naloxone (Narcan)** | A medication that rapidly reverses opioid overdose. Available over-the-counter in Missouri pharmacies without a prescription. Can be administered as a nasal spray. |
| **MAT (Medication-Assisted Treatment)** | Use of FDA-approved medications (buprenorphine/Suboxone, methadone, naltrexone/Vivitrol) combined with counseling to treat opioid and alcohol use disorders. |
| **Detoxification (Detox)** | Medically supervised withdrawal management. Detox alone is not treatment — it must be followed by ongoing care. |
| **Co-occurring disorder** | When a person has both a mental health condition and a substance use disorder simultaneously. Requires integrated treatment. |
| **Baker Act equivalent** | Missouri does not use the term "Baker Act" (that is Florida). Missouri's equivalent is the 96-hour hold under RSMo § 632.305. |
| **SBIRT** | Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment — a framework used in healthcare settings to identify and address substance use early. |

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Opioid Overdose Response

**Signs of overdose:** Very small (pinpoint) pupils, slow or stopped breathing, blue lips or fingertips, unresponsive to noise or pain, gurgling or snoring sounds.

1. **Call 911 immediately.** Say "Someone is not breathing" or "I think someone is overdosing."
2. **Administer naloxone (Narcan) if available:**
   - Nasal spray: Insert nozzle into one nostril, press plunger firmly
   - If no response in 2–3 minutes, administer a second dose in the other nostril
3. **Begin rescue breathing or CPR** if the person is not breathing.
4. **Place the person on their side** (recovery position) to prevent choking.
5. **Stay with the person** until EMS arrives. Naloxone wears off in 30–90 minutes — the person may stop breathing again.
6. **Missouri's Drug Overdose Immunity Law (RSMo § 195.205):** You have limited protection from prosecution for drug possession when you call 911 to report an overdose. Do not let fear of arrest stop you from calling.

### Procedure 2: Someone Is Expressing Suicidal Thoughts

1. **Take it seriously.** Every mention of suicide deserves a response.
2. **Ask directly:** "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Asking does not increase risk.
3. **Listen without judgment.** Do not argue, debate, or minimize.
4. **Assess for plan and means:**
   - "Do you have a plan for how you would do it?"
   - "Do you have access to [means]?"
   - If yes to both: this is **high risk** — call 988 or 911.
5. **Remove means if safe to do so.** Secure firearms, medications, sharp objects.
6. **Connect to help:** Call or text **988**, or call BHR at **314-469-6644**.
7. **Do not leave the person alone** until they are connected to a crisis professional.
8. **Follow up.** Check in within 24–48 hours. The period after a crisis is high-risk.

### Procedure 3: Psychiatric Hold (96-Hour Hold) in Missouri

**Who can initiate:**
- A peace officer (police, sheriff)
- A licensed physician
- A licensed mental health professional (psychologist, LCSW, LPC with appropriate authority)

**Criteria (RSMo § 632.305):**
The person must present a likelihood of serious harm to themselves or others due to a mental disorder AND be unable or unwilling to seek voluntary treatment.

**Process:**
1. The person is taken to an approved psychiatric facility (see list above).
2. A physician or mental health professional evaluates the person within the first few hours.
3. The person may be held up to **96 hours** for evaluation and stabilization.
4. Before 96 hours, the facility must either:
   - Release the person, or
   - File for a court-ordered continued detention (judicial review required)
5. The person has the right to:
   - Be informed of the reason for detention
   - Contact an attorney
   - A judicial hearing if detention is extended beyond 96 hours
   - Refuse medication except in emergencies

**For families:** If you believe a loved one needs a psychiatric hold, call BHR (314-469-6644) or 911. You cannot directly initiate a hold — only authorized professionals can. But you can request a wellness check and explain your concerns to responders.

### Procedure 4: Accessing Substance Use Treatment

1. **For immediate crisis (overdose or danger):** Call 911.
2. **For non-emergency help starting treatment:**
   - Call SAMHSA National Helpline: **1-800-662-4357** (free referral, 24/7)
   - Call NCADA for local assessment: **314-962-3456**
3. **Types of treatment available:**
   - **Detox:** Medically supervised withdrawal (3–7 days typically)
   - **Residential:** Structured live-in treatment (28 days to 6+ months)
   - **Intensive outpatient (IOP):** Group therapy 3–5 days/week while living at home
   - **Outpatient:** Individual and group counseling 1–2 times/week
   - **MAT:** Medication (buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone) combined with counseling
4. **Insurance and cost:**
   - Missouri Medicaid (MO HealthNet) covers SUD treatment including MAT
   - Most facilities accept Medicaid, private insurance, and offer sliding-scale fees
   - SAMHSA helpline can identify free/low-cost options

## Behavioral Health and Domestic Violence

Abuse often co-occurs with mental health and substance use crises. Important considerations:

- **Abusers may weaponize mental health:** Using a partner's diagnosis, medication, or hospitalization as a tool of control ("You're crazy," threatening to report them as unfit parents).
- **Survivors may self-medicate:** Substance use can be a coping mechanism for trauma. Do not treat it as the primary problem without addressing safety.
- **Psychiatric holds can be misused:** Abusers sometimes call police claiming a partner is suicidal to get them detained. If a person reports this, take it seriously and document it.
- **Suicidal threats by an abuser:** If an abuser threatens suicide to prevent a partner from leaving, this is a form of coercive control. The survivor should call 988 or 911 on the abuser's behalf and proceed with their safety plan.
- **Children's behavioral health:** Children exposed to domestic violence often present with anxiety, depression, PTSD, aggression, or regression. Connect to children's mental health services (see Crisis Response pod, Procedure 5).

## Missouri Legal Framework

| Statute | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **RSMo § 632.305** | 96-hour involuntary psychiatric hold — imminent danger to self or others due to mental disorder |
| **RSMo § 632.300** | Voluntary admission to psychiatric facilities; right to request discharge within 3 days |
| **RSMo § 195.205** | Drug Overdose Immunity — limited protection from drug possession charges when calling 911 for overdose |
| **RSMo § 630.140** | Confidentiality of mental health records; cannot be disclosed without written consent except in limited circumstances |
| **RSMo § 630.167** | Rights of persons receiving mental health services (right to treatment, least restrictive environment, informed consent) |
