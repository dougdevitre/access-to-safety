## Overview

Environmental safety covers the physical conditions that affect a person's health and safety: housing quality, workplace hazards, natural disasters, and environmental exposures like lead and mold. For survivors of abuse, environmental safety intersects with their situation in critical ways — unsafe housing may be the only housing available, an abuser may use housing instability as leverage, and disasters can destroy the fragile safety nets survivors depend on.

This pod provides guidance on housing code violations, disaster preparedness (especially relevant for St. Louis County's tornado and flood risk), workplace safety rights, and environmental health hazards.

## Key Concepts and Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Code violation** | A condition in a rental property or building that violates local building, health, or safety codes — such as no heat, broken locks, pest infestation, exposed wiring, or structural damage. |
| **Implied warranty of habitability** | A legal principle (recognized in Missouri) that landlords must maintain rental properties in a livable condition. Tenants may have legal remedies if this is violated. |
| **Lead paint** | Lead-based paint was banned in 1978 but remains in many older homes. Lead exposure is especially dangerous for children under 6 and pregnant women. STL County has a high rate of older housing stock. |
| **Mold** | Toxic mold (such as black mold / Stachybotrys) can cause respiratory illness, allergic reactions, and worsen asthma. Landlords are generally responsible for addressing moisture and mold issues. |
| **OSHA** | Occupational Safety and Health Administration — the federal agency responsible for workplace safety standards and enforcement. |
| **Tornado Watch** | Conditions are favorable for tornadoes. Stay alert and ready to shelter. |
| **Tornado Warning** | A tornado has been spotted or indicated on radar. Take shelter immediately. |
| **Flood stage** | The water level at which a river begins to overflow its banks. The Meramec and Missouri rivers in the STL area have periodic major flooding. |

## Housing Safety

### Unsafe Housing: Know Your Rights as a Tenant in Missouri

**Landlords in Missouri must provide:**
- Working plumbing, heating, and electrical systems
- Structural integrity (no collapsing ceilings, floors, or walls)
- Working smoke detectors (RSMo § 320.106)
- Working carbon monoxide detectors in dwellings with fuel-burning appliances (RSMo § 320.106)
- Locks on exterior doors and windows
- Freedom from pest infestation
- Compliance with local building and health codes

**If your landlord is not maintaining your home:**

1. **Document everything:** Photograph or video the conditions. Note dates.
2. **Notify your landlord in writing.** Send a letter or email describing the problem and requesting repair. Keep a copy.
3. **Contact your local code enforcement:**
   - St. Louis County Code Enforcement: **314-615-4220**
   - Individual municipalities may have their own code enforcement offices
4. **Contact Legal Aid:**
   - Legal Services of Eastern Missouri: **314-534-4200** (free legal help for qualifying residents)
   - Legal Aid of Western Missouri: **816-474-6750**
5. **Know your options:**
   - **Rent escrow (RSMo § 441.570–441.580):** If a code violation exists and the landlord has been notified, you may deposit rent with the court instead of paying the landlord, until repairs are made.
   - **Repair and deduct:** In some circumstances, tenants may make minor repairs and deduct the cost from rent. Consult a legal aid attorney before doing this.
   - **Constructive eviction:** If conditions make the home uninhabitable, you may be able to break the lease without penalty. Get legal advice first.

**Retaliation protections:** Missouri law (RSMo § 441.233) prohibits landlords from retaliating (eviction, rent increase, service reduction) against tenants who report code violations or exercise legal rights.

### For Survivors of Abuse: Housing-Specific Concerns

- **If your abuser is your landlord:** Contact a DV advocate immediately. You may have additional legal protections. National DV Hotline: 1-800-799-7233.
- **If your abuser controls the lease:** Missouri law (RSMo § 441.920) allows survivors of DV, sexual assault, or stalking to terminate a lease early by providing a protective order or police report, with 30 days' notice. No early termination penalty.
- **If you need emergency shelter:** See the Resource Directory (templates/resource-directory.md) for shelter listings, or call the National DV Hotline.
- **Safe at Home Program (Missouri Secretary of State):** Provides a substitute address for survivors to use on public records, voter registration, and other documents. Phone: **866-509-1409**.

### Lead Paint

**Who is at risk:** Children under 6 and pregnant women are most vulnerable. Homes built before 1978 may contain lead paint.

**Signs of lead exposure in children:** Developmental delays, learning difficulties, irritability, loss of appetite, weight loss, fatigue, abdominal pain, vomiting.

**What to do:**
1. **Get your child tested.** Ask your pediatrician for a blood lead level test. Missouri law requires testing at ages 1 and 2 for children in high-risk areas.
2. **Report lead hazards:**
   - St. Louis County Dept. of Public Health: **314-615-0600**
   - Missouri Dept. of Health and Senior Services Lead Program: **573-751-6102**
3. **Do NOT attempt to remove lead paint yourself.** Improper removal creates lead dust, which is more dangerous than intact paint. Landlords are legally required to address lead hazards in rental properties.
4. **Free resources:** The STL County Health Department offers free lead inspections for homes with children who have elevated blood lead levels.

### Mold

1. **Document the mold:** Photograph it with a ruler or coin for scale.
2. **Notify your landlord in writing.**
3. **Report to code enforcement** if the landlord does not respond within a reasonable time.
4. **Health impacts:** If you are experiencing respiratory symptoms, see a healthcare provider and mention the mold exposure.
5. **Small areas (< 10 sq ft):** Can often be cleaned with detergent and water. Wear a mask.
6. **Large areas or recurring mold:** Requires professional remediation. This is the landlord's responsibility in rental properties.

## Disaster Preparedness (St. Louis County)

### Tornado Safety

St. Louis County is in the central U.S. tornado corridor. Peak season is March through June.

**Before a tornado:**
- Identify your shelter location: basement, interior room on lowest floor, away from windows
- Know the difference: **Watch** = conditions favorable; **Warning** = take shelter NOW
- Have an emergency kit ready (see below)
- Sign up for STL County emergency alerts: stlouisco.com/emergency-management

**During a tornado WARNING:**
1. Go to your shelter location immediately
2. Get under a sturdy piece of furniture
3. Protect your head and neck
4. If in a mobile home: leave and go to the nearest sturdy building or lie flat in a ditch
5. If driving: do NOT shelter under an overpass; pull over and lie flat in a ditch or get to a sturdy building

**After a tornado:**
- Check yourself and others for injuries
- Do not enter damaged buildings
- Watch for downed power lines and gas leaks
- Call 911 if anyone is injured or trapped
- Contact Red Cross: **1-800-733-2767** for emergency shelter and assistance

### Flood Safety

The Meramec River, Missouri River, and their tributaries can flood with heavy rain. Flash flooding is the most dangerous.

**During a flood:**
- **Never walk, swim, or drive through flood water.** "Turn around, don't drown." Six inches of moving water can knock you down. Two feet can carry away a vehicle.
- Move to higher ground immediately if water is rising
- If trapped in a building: go to the highest floor; do not go into the attic unless you have roof access
- Call 911 if you are trapped

**After a flood:**
- Do not return home until authorities say it is safe
- Do not drink tap water until it has been declared safe
- Photograph all damage before cleaning for insurance purposes
- Contact FEMA for disaster assistance: **1-800-621-3362** or DisasterAssistance.gov

### Emergency Kit Checklist

Keep these items in an accessible location:

- [ ] Water: 1 gallon per person per day (3-day minimum)
- [ ] Non-perishable food (3-day supply) and manual can opener
- [ ] Battery-powered or hand-crank radio (NOAA weather radio)
- [ ] Flashlight and extra batteries
- [ ] First aid kit
- [ ] Prescription medications (7-day supply)
- [ ] Phone charger (portable battery pack)
- [ ] Cash (small bills — ATMs may not work)
- [ ] Copies of important documents in waterproof container
- [ ] Change of clothes and sturdy shoes
- [ ] Blankets or sleeping bags
- [ ] Whistle (to signal for help)
- [ ] Dust masks, plastic sheeting, and duct tape (for sheltering in place)

**For survivors with a go bag:** Your safety plan go bag (see safety-plan.md, Section 9) can double as your emergency kit. Keep both ready.

## Workplace Safety

### Your Rights Under OSHA

Every worker has the right to:
- A safe workplace free from recognized hazards
- Information and training about workplace hazards in a language you understand
- Report unsafe conditions without retaliation
- Request an OSHA inspection
- Receive copies of workplace injury and illness records

**To report a workplace safety hazard:**
- File online: osha.gov/workers/file-complaint
- Call OSHA's St. Louis Area Office: **314-425-4249**
- Call OSHA toll-free: **1-800-321-OSHA (6742)**

**Retaliation protection:** It is illegal for your employer to fire, demote, or punish you for reporting safety concerns or filing an OSHA complaint (Section 11(c) of the OSH Act).

### For Survivors: Workplace Safety Considerations

- **If your abuser shows up at your workplace:** Inform a trusted coworker or supervisor. Ask building security to be alerted. See safety-plan.md Section 6.
- **Workplace protective orders:** Missouri allows employers to seek workplace restraining orders on behalf of employees (RSMo § 455.075).
- **Time off for court:** Missouri law allows employees to take time off to attend court for protective orders or criminal proceedings related to domestic or sexual violence without employer retaliation.
