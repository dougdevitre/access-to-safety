## Overview

Every population served by Access to Safety faces barriers, but some groups face compounding barriers that standard safety resources do not adequately address. An undocumented immigrant may fear deportation if they call police. A deaf survivor may not be able to call a crisis hotline. A transgender person may be turned away from a gendered shelter. A veteran may distrust civilian mental health systems. A youth aging out of foster care may have no family safety net at all.

This pod provides population-specific guidance, resources, and considerations that layer on top of the other pods. When someone from a vulnerable population needs help, consult this pod **in addition to** the relevant topic pod (e.g., Pod 2 for DV + Pod 8 for immigration-specific guidance).

## LGBTQ+ Survivors

### Unique Barriers

- **Fear of outing:** Seeking help may force a person to disclose their sexual orientation or gender identity to police, courts, family, or employers.
- **Gendered shelters:** Many DV shelters serve "women and children only," which may exclude transgender women, transgender men, and non-binary individuals. Ask shelters directly about their policies.
- **Mutual abuse myths:** When both partners are the same gender, police and courts may dismiss the situation as "mutual fighting" rather than recognizing a primary aggressor.
- **Isolation:** LGBTQ+ individuals may have smaller support networks, especially if estranged from family of origin.
- **Legal gaps:** Missouri does not have statewide LGBTQ+ non-discrimination protections in housing or employment. Some municipalities (St. Louis City, University City, Clayton) have local ordinances.
- **IPV rates:** LGBTQ+ individuals experience intimate partner violence at equal or higher rates than heterosexual/cisgender individuals, but access services at lower rates.

### Guidance for Advocates Working with LGBTQ+ Survivors

- **Use the name and pronouns the person tells you.** Do not refer to a legal name they have not offered.
- **Do not assume the gender of the abuser.** Use the language the survivor uses.
- **Screen shelters before referring.** Call ahead and ask: "Do you accept transgender women/men? What is your policy for non-binary individuals?"
- **Protective orders are available regardless of gender or sexual orientation.** Missouri's Full Order of Protection (RSMo § 455.010–455.085) covers "any person who is or has been in a continuing social relationship of a romantic or intimate nature" — this includes same-sex partners.
- **Document anti-LGBTQ+ bias** if it is a factor in the abuse (e.g., threatening to out the survivor, using slurs, weaponizing a person's identity). This can support a hate crime report if applicable.

## Immigrants and Refugees

### Unique Barriers

- **Fear of deportation:** Undocumented survivors fear that any contact with government systems (police, courts, hospitals) may lead to deportation. Abusers exploit this fear.
- **Language access:** Limited English proficiency creates barriers to understanding rights, navigating systems, and communicating with law enforcement.
- **Document control:** Abusers frequently confiscate passports, visas, and immigration documents as a control tactic. This is a federal crime when done for labor or sex trafficking.
- **Economic dependence:** Immigration status may be tied to the abuser's sponsorship, making the survivor economically dependent.
- **Cultural barriers:** Shame, family pressure, and community standing may discourage reporting.
- **System distrust:** Experiences with law enforcement or government in countries of origin may create deep distrust of U.S. systems.

### Immigration-Specific Legal Protections

| Protection | What It Is | Key Details |
|------------|-----------|-------------|
| **U-visa** | For victims of qualifying crimes (DV, sexual assault, trafficking, stalking, etc.) who assist law enforcement | Provides temporary legal status and work authorization. Requires law enforcement certification (Form I-918B). Up to 10,000 issued per year. Path to green card after 3 years. |
| **T-visa** | For victims of human trafficking | Provides temporary legal status, work authorization, and access to federal benefits. Does not require law enforcement cooperation (but cooperation is considered). Path to green card. |
| **VAWA Self-Petition** | For spouses, children, and parents of abusive U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents | Allows the survivor to petition for legal status independently, without the abuser's knowledge or involvement. |
| **Asylum** | For persons fleeing persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group | DV survivors may qualify if the abuse is connected to a protected ground and the home country cannot or will not protect them. Must file within 1 year of arrival (with exceptions). |
| **Special Immigrant Juvenile Status (SIJS)** | For children under 21 who have been abused, neglected, or abandoned by a parent | Provides path to green card through juvenile court finding and USCIS petition. |

### Guidance for Advocates

- **You are not required to ask about immigration status** when providing services. Many DV organizations have policies against asking.
- **Explain that police are not ICE.** Local law enforcement in St. Louis County is generally not involved in immigration enforcement. However, this varies — be honest about risks.
- **Connect to immigration legal help early.** U-visa applications require law enforcement certification, which has a time-sensitive element. Start the process as soon as possible.
- **Use interpreters, not family members.** Family members may be unsafe (could be the abuser or connected to the abuser). Use professional interpretation services.
- **Reassure about public benefits:** Crime victims accessing services (shelters, medical care, hotlines) are generally not subject to "public charge" determinations for immigration purposes.

## People with Disabilities

### Unique Barriers

- **Physical dependence:** A survivor with a disability may depend on the abuser for mobility, medication administration, communication, or daily living tasks.
- **Communication barriers:** Deaf/hard-of-hearing, non-verbal, or cognitively disabled survivors may not be able to use standard hotlines or communicate effectively with police.
- **Institutional abuse:** Abuse may occur in group homes, assisted living facilities, or by paid caregivers — not just intimate partners.
- **Credibility bias:** Survivors with intellectual or psychiatric disabilities may not be believed by police, courts, or service providers.
- **Inaccessible services:** Shelters, courthouses, and advocacy offices may lack wheelchair access, ASL interpreters, or accommodations for sensory or cognitive disabilities.
- **Guardianship abuse:** A legal guardian may be the abuser, controlling finances, medical decisions, and movement.

### Accessibility Checklist for Service Providers

When referring a survivor with a disability, verify with the receiving organization:

- [ ] Is the facility wheelchair accessible (entrance, restrooms, sleeping areas)?
- [ ] Are ASL interpreters available (on-site or via VRI)?
- [ ] Can they accommodate service animals?
- [ ] Do they have accessible communication materials (large print, Braille, plain language)?
- [ ] Can they accommodate medication schedules and medical equipment (power for CPAP, oxygen, etc.)?
- [ ] Will they accept a personal care attendant with the survivor?
- [ ] Is the intake process adapted for survivors with intellectual or developmental disabilities?

## Veterans

### Unique Barriers

- **Military culture:** "Handle it yourself" attitudes may discourage help-seeking. Chain-of-command mindset may normalize control.
- **PTSD and TBI:** Combat-related trauma can co-occur with IPV — as either a contributing factor or an additional complexity for a survivor who is a veteran.
- **Military justice vs. civilian:** If the abuser is active-duty military, the survivor may need to navigate both military and civilian systems.
- **Economic entanglement:** Military benefits (housing, healthcare, dependent pay) may be tied to the abusive partner's service status.
- **Firearms access:** Active-duty and veteran service members have higher rates of firearm ownership, which increases lethality risk in DV situations.

| **Military OneSource** | **800-342-9647** | Confidential support for active duty, reserves, and families |

### For Survivors Whose Abuser Is Active-Duty Military

- **Military Protective Orders (MPOs):** A commanding officer can issue an MPO ordering the service member to stay away. This is separate from a civilian protective order and applies only on military installations.
- **Civilian protective orders still apply.** You can file for a civilian protective order regardless of the abuser's military status. Military personnel are subject to both civilian and UCMJ (Uniform Code of Military Justice).
- **Family Advocacy Program (FAP):** Each military installation has a FAP that addresses domestic abuse within military families. Contact the installation's FAP office.
- **Restricted vs. unrestricted reporting:** Active-duty survivors can make a "restricted report" (confidential, no investigation triggered) or an "unrestricted report" (triggers investigation). Civilian survivors reporting active-duty abusers go through civilian channels.

## Unhoused Persons

### Unique Barriers

- **Survival takes priority:** When someone is focused on finding food and shelter tonight, safety planning and legal remedies feel inaccessible.
- **Lack of address:** Many services, benefits, and legal processes require a mailing address.
- **Criminalization:** Unhoused individuals face higher rates of police contact, which can create distrust and fear of seeking help.
- **Invisibility:** DV among unhoused populations is underreported and under-studied.
- **Shelter barriers:** DV shelters may have rules (sobriety requirements, curfews) that conflict with the realities of homelessness.

### For Survivors Experiencing Homelessness

- **You are eligible for DV services** even if you are unhoused. Homelessness does not disqualify you from shelter, advocacy, legal aid, or protective orders.
- **Safe at Home (Missouri):** If you obtain housing, you can use a substitute address on public records. Phone: **866-509-1409**.
- **P.O. Box access:** Some organizations provide free P.O. Box access or mail services for unhoused individuals. Ask at St. Patrick Center.

## Youth Aging Out of Foster Care

### Unique Barriers

- **No family safety net:** Youth who age out at 18 (or 21 with extended foster care) often have no family to call in a crisis.
- **Housing instability:** The transition from foster care to independent living is the highest-risk period for homelessness.
- **Targeting by traffickers:** Youth aging out are disproportionately targeted for labor and sex trafficking.
- **Limited system knowledge:** They may not know what resources exist or how to access them.
- **Trust issues:** A lifetime in "the system" may create deep distrust of service providers and authority figures.

### Resources for Transitioning Youth

| Resource | Contact | Services |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **Foster Care to Success** | fc2success.org | Scholarships, care packages, coaching for foster care alumni |
| **National Runaway Safeline** | **1-800-786-2929** | Crisis support for youth; "Home Free" bus ticket program |
| **Covenant House** | covenanthouse.org | Shelter and services for homeless youth (16–24) |
| **Doorways (STL)** | doorwayshousing.org | Housing for youth experiencing homelessness (16–24), including LGBTQ+ youth |
| **Missouri Chafee Program** | dss.mo.gov (search Chafee) | Independent living services, education vouchers, transition support for current/former foster youth |
| **National Human Trafficking Hotline** | **1-888-373-7888** | If a youth is being exploited |

### For Advocates Working with Transition-Age Youth

- **Meet them where they are.** Literally — youth in this population may not have reliable transportation, phone service, or a stable address. Offer to meet at a library, coffee shop, or community center.
- **Build trust slowly.** They may have had dozens of caseworkers. Consistency matters more than efficiency.
- **Screen for trafficking.** Youth aging out of care are at very high risk. Ask about their living situation, employment, and whether anyone is controlling their money or movements.
- **Connect to Chafee benefits.** Many eligible youth do not know about them.

## Tribal Communities

### Unique Barriers

- **Jurisdictional complexity:** Crimes on tribal land may fall under tribal, federal, or state jurisdiction depending on the crime and the identities of the parties. This can delay or complicate response.
- **Limited resources:** Many tribal communities have under-resourced law enforcement and social services.
- **Cultural considerations:** Responses should respect tribal sovereignty, cultural practices, and community structures.
- **Historical trauma:** Generational trauma from colonization, boarding schools, and forced family separation affects trust in outside systems.

### Tribal-Specific Resources

| Resource | Contact | Services |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **StrongHearts Native Helpline** | **1-844-762-8483** | DV and SA support for Native Americans and Alaska Natives; chat at strongheartshelpline.org |
| **National Indigenous Women's Resource Center** | niwrc.org | Policy, advocacy, and resources for Native survivors |
| **Bureau of Indian Affairs — Indian Country Victim Services** | bia.gov | Federal victim services coordination |

> **Note:** Missouri does not currently have federally recognized tribal reservations within its borders, but Native American residents of St. Louis County may identify with tribal communities elsewhere and benefit from culturally specific resources.

## Cross-Cutting Considerations for All Vulnerable Populations

### Language Access

- **Federal law requires** recipients of federal funding to provide meaningful language access (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act).
- **Interpreter services:** Never use children, family members, or the abuser as interpreters. Use professional interpretation (in-person, phone, or video).
- **Language line:** Many organizations contract with language services like LanguageLine Solutions (accessible by phone in 240+ languages). Ask the receiving organization if this is available.

### Intersectionality

Many survivors belong to more than one vulnerable group (e.g., an undocumented transgender woman, a deaf veteran, a homeless youth with a disability). When multiple identities intersect:

- **Layer the guidance** from multiple sections of this pod.
- **Do not prioritize one identity over another** — address the full picture.
- **Ask the survivor** which barriers feel most pressing to them.
- **Seek specialized referrals** — organizations that serve intersecting populations (e.g., immigration + DV, LGBTQ+ + youth homelessness) are more effective than generalist services.
