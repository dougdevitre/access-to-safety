## Overview

Crisis response covers the full spectrum of immediate help available when someone is in danger or experiencing a mental health, behavioral health, or safety emergency. In St. Louis County, multiple systems work in parallel: law enforcement dispatch (911), the mental health crisis line and 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, mobile crisis teams, co-responder units, and crisis stabilization units. Understanding which door to open — and when — can save lives and connect people to the right level of care.

This pod focuses on the acute response phase: the first hours of a crisis. For longer-term behavioral health support, see the **Behavioral Health Crisis** pod. For violence-specific crises, see the **Violence & Abuse** pod.

## Key Concepts and Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **911** | Universal emergency number for police, fire, and EMS. Dispatches law enforcement by default unless a specialized unit is available. |
| **988** | National Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (since July 2022). Call or text 988. Connects to Missouri's crisis network. |
| **BHR (Behavioral Health Response)** | St. Louis County's 24/7 mobile crisis dispatch line. Routes non-emergency mental health calls to clinicians instead of police. Phone: **314-469-6644**. |
| **Mobile Crisis Team (MCT)** | A team of behavioral health clinicians — sometimes paired with a peer support specialist — who respond in the field to mental health crises without police. |
| **Co-Responder Model** | A licensed clinician rides alongside or responds with a law enforcement officer. Reduces use of force and increases connections to treatment. STL County has co-responder units in some precincts. |
| **Crisis Stabilization Unit (CSU)** | A short-term facility where people in crisis can stabilize without hospital admission. Less restrictive than a psychiatric emergency room. |
| **CIT (Crisis Intervention Training)** | A 40-hour training curriculum for law enforcement covering mental health recognition, de-escalation, and diversion. Many STL County officers are CIT-certified. |
| **Psychiatric Hold / 96-Hour Hold** | Under Missouri RSMo § 632.305, a person can be involuntarily detained up to 96 hours for evaluation when they pose imminent danger to themselves or others. See Behavioral Health Crisis pod. |
| **Warm Handoff** | A live, real-time transfer of care from one provider to another — for example, a crisis counselor staying on the line until EMS arrives. |
| **Peer Support Specialist** | A person with lived experience of mental health or substance use challenges, trained and certified to support others in crisis. Often embedded in mobile crisis teams. |
| **Diversion Program** | An alternative pathway that steers people away from arrest or emergency rooms toward community-based care. |

## The St. Louis County Crisis Response System

### How 988 Works in Missouri

1. A person calls or texts **988**.
2. The call routes to Missouri's designated crisis center network, managed by the Missouri Department of Mental Health.
3. A trained crisis counselor answers — typically within 60 seconds.
4. The counselor assesses risk, provides support, and can dispatch mobile crisis or coordinate with BHR.
5. If imminent danger exists, the counselor can coordinate a 911 dispatch or a warm handoff to EMS.
6. Follow-up calls may be placed within 24–72 hours.

**Spanish language support:** Press 2 for Spanish. Additional languages available through interpretation services.
**Veterans:** Press 1 after dialing.
**LGBTQ+ youth:** Separate support available via Trevor Project (1-866-488-7386) or text START to 678-678.

### How BHR Works (314-469-6644)

BHR is St. Louis County's primary alternative to sending police to a mental health call. It is operated by the Behavioral Health Network of Greater St. Louis.

1. A caller dials **314-469-6644** or is transferred from 911 or 988.
2. A licensed clinician triages the call.
3. Depending on risk level, BHR can:
   - Provide phone-based crisis counseling
   - Dispatch a mobile crisis team to the scene
   - Coordinate a warm handoff to emergency services
   - Connect the person to crisis stabilization or outpatient resources
4. Mobile crisis teams typically respond within 30–60 minutes (varies by availability and location).
5. Teams do not carry weapons and are not accompanied by police unless safety concerns require it.

**When to call BHR instead of 911:**
- Person is expressing suicidal thoughts but is not in immediate physical danger
- Person is experiencing a mental health episode (psychosis, mania, severe anxiety) without weapons or active violence
- Person needs a wellness check but is not believed to be in immediate danger
- Family member needs guidance on how to help a loved one in crisis

**When to call 911 instead of or in addition to BHR:**
- Weapons are present or have been used
- Someone has already been physically harmed
- The person is unconscious or unresponsive
- There is an immediate threat to life

### Co-Responder Program (STL County)

St. Louis County Police Department has embedded licensed mental health clinicians in selected precinct units. These clinicians:
- Ride along or respond to scenes alongside officers
- Conduct on-scene mental health assessment
- Connect individuals to services and diversion programs
- Prepare documentation to support involuntary holds when clinically indicated
- Serve as a bridge between law enforcement and the behavioral health system

Contact the STL County Police Department's Community Outreach Unit for co-responder inquiries: **314-889-2341**.

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Someone Is Expressing Suicidal Thoughts

1. **Stay calm and stay with the person** (physically or by phone).
2. **Ask directly:** "Are you thinking about suicide?" — Asking does not increase risk; it opens a door.
3. **Listen without judgment.** Do not argue, minimize, or make promises you cannot keep.
4. **Remove means if safe to do so** — medications, firearms, sharp objects. Ask: "Can we put the [item] somewhere else for now?"
5. **Call 988** or help them call: dial or text **988**.
6. **If immediate danger exists** — the person has a plan, means, and intent — call **911**.
7. **Do not leave the person alone** until crisis support has arrived or been connected.
8. **After the crisis:** Follow up. Share local resources. See Behavioral Health Crisis pod for next steps.

### Procedure 2: Requesting a Mobile Crisis Team

1. Call **BHR at 314-469-6644**.
2. Be ready to share:
   - Location (address or nearest intersection)
   - Description of what is happening
   - Whether weapons are present
   - Person's name and age if known
   - Any known mental health diagnosis or medications
3. BHR will triage and dispatch if appropriate.
4. If the situation escalates before the team arrives, call **911** and tell dispatch that a mental health crisis team was already called.
5. When the team arrives, let them lead. You may be asked to step back.

### Procedure 3: Requesting a Wellness Check

1. For a wellness check involving mental health concerns: call **BHR 314-469-6644** first — they may be able to send a non-law-enforcement responder.
2. If BHR cannot respond and the concern is urgent: call **STL County Police non-emergency at 314-889-2341**.
3. If you believe the person is in immediate danger: call **911**.
4. Provide the person's address, physical description, and the reason for concern.
5. Ask dispatch if a CIT-trained officer or co-responder is available.

### Procedure 4: Helping Someone in Substance Use Crisis (Not Overdose)

1. For overdose, call **911 immediately**. See Behavioral Health Crisis pod for naloxone/overdose response.
2. For a substance use crisis without overdose, call **BHR 314-469-6644** or **SAMHSA 1-800-662-4357**.
3. Keep the person safe, calm, and away from additional substances.
4. Do not physically restrain unless absolutely necessary for immediate safety.
5. Missouri's **Drug Overdose Immunity Law (RSMo § 195.205)** provides limited immunity from prosecution when calling 911 to report an overdose — this may reduce fear of calling for help.

### Procedure 5: Supporting a Child in Crisis

1. For a child in immediate danger: call **911**.
2. For a child in mental health crisis: call **BHR 314-469-6644** — they have clinicians trained in pediatric crisis response.
3. St. Louis Children's Hospital has a dedicated psychiatric emergency service: **314-454-6000**.
4. For suspected abuse or neglect, call the **Missouri Child Abuse Hotline: 1-800-392-3738** (see Violence & Abuse pod).
5. Do not leave a child in crisis alone. Stay with them or ensure a trusted adult is present.

## Crisis Stabilization and Diversion Options in STL County

### Crisis Stabilization Units (CSUs)

CSUs offer short-term, voluntary stabilization — typically up to 23 hours — without hospital admission. They are lower-cost and less traumatizing than emergency rooms for many people in crisis.

| Facility | Phone | Notes |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Behavioral Health Network (BHN) Crisis Center | 314-469-6644 (via BHR) | Referral-based; accessed through BHR dispatch |
| Compass Health Network | 636-946-0790 | Outpatient and crisis services, multiple STL area locations |

### Diversion Programs

- **Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion (LEAD):** In some STL County jurisdictions, officers can divert people from arrest toward social services for low-level offenses driven by behavioral health needs.
- **Mental Health Court (21st Judicial Circuit):** A specialty court for individuals with serious mental illness charged with nonviolent offenses. Contact: St. Louis County Circuit Court, **314-615-8000**.
- **Drug Court:** Similar structure for substance use-related offenses. Contact the same circuit court number above.

## When to Escalate / Emergency Indicators

**Call 911 immediately if:**
- Someone is actively harming themselves or others
- A person is unconscious, not breathing, or having a medical emergency alongside the crisis
- Weapons are present or have been used
- A child is in immediate physical danger
- The person is threatening specific, imminent harm to an identifiable person
- A crisis team has been dispatched but the situation deteriorates before they arrive

**Indicators of high suicide risk (escalate to 911 or emergency psychiatric evaluation):**
- The person has a specific plan ("I'm going to take all my pills tonight")
- They have access to means (firearm, stockpiled medications)
- A recent serious attempt
- Saying goodbye or giving away possessions
- Sudden calmness after severe distress — may mean a decision has been made

**Indicators of psychiatric emergency (call BHR or 911):**
- Person cannot recognize reality (hallucinations, severe delusions)
- Person cannot meet basic needs (not eating, cannot stay safe)
- Person poses danger to others due to psychiatric symptoms

## Missouri Legal Framework

| Statute | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **RSMo § 632.305** | Authorizes 96-hour involuntary psychiatric holds. A peace officer, physician, or mental health professional may take someone into protective custody if they pose imminent danger due to a mental disorder. |
| **RSMo § 632.300** | Governs voluntary admission to mental health facilities. |
| **RSMo § 195.205** | Drug Overdose Immunity — limited protection from prosecution when calling 911 for an overdose. |
| **RSMo § 217.735** | Missouri's Mental Health Diversion Program framework. |
