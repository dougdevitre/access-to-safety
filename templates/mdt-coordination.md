# Multi-Disciplinary Team (MDT) Coordination Template

> **AI Instructions:** When an advocate asks about coordinating with other agencies, warm
> handoffs, multi-disciplinary teams, coordinated community response, case conferencing,
> or inter-agency collaboration, use this template to provide structure and guidance. MDT
> work is critical in DV cases because survivors interact with many systems simultaneously.
> Always remind advocates that information sharing across agencies requires survivor consent
> and must comply with confidentiality requirements (VAWA confidentiality provisions,
> HIPAA where applicable, and state law).

---

## What Is a Multi-Disciplinary Team (MDT)?

A Multi-Disciplinary Team is a group of professionals from different agencies and disciplines
who meet regularly to coordinate services for survivors of domestic violence, sexual
assault, child abuse, or other forms of interpersonal violence.

**Common MDT members:**
- DV advocates / shelter staff
- Law enforcement (detective, victim unit officer)
- Prosecutors (victim/witness coordinators, assistant prosecutors)
- Child protective services (CPS / Children's Division)
- Medical professionals (SANE nurses, ER staff, pediatricians)
- Mental health providers (therapists, crisis counselors)
- Legal aid attorneys
- Immigration attorneys
- Housing services
- School counselors or liaisons
- Probation/parole officers
- Batterer intervention program staff
- Faith community representatives

**Why it matters:** Without coordination, survivors are forced to retell their story to
every agency, navigate conflicting advice, and fall through the cracks between systems.
Coordinated response improves safety outcomes, reduces re-traumatization, and holds
offenders accountable more effectively.

## Confidentiality — The Non-Negotiable Foundation

**Before sharing ANY information across agencies:**

1. **Get written, informed, time-limited consent from the survivor.** The consent form must specify:
   - What information will be shared
   - With whom it will be shared
   - For what purpose
   - For how long (expiration date)
   - The survivor's right to revoke consent at any time

2. **Know the legal framework:**
   - **VAWA confidentiality provisions** (42 U.S.C. § 13925(b)(2)) — Federally funded DV programs cannot share survivor PII without informed, written, reasonably time-limited consent
   - **HIPAA** — Medical providers cannot share health information without authorization
   - **State confidentiality laws** — May provide additional protections (check your state)
   - **Attorney-client privilege** — Attorneys cannot share client information without consent
   - **Exceptions:** Mandatory reporting of child abuse, imminent danger to self/others, court orders

3. **Minimum necessary principle.** Share only the information needed for the specific purpose consented to. Not everything you know — only what the partner agency needs to fulfill their role.

4. **Document all disclosures.** Record what was shared, with whom, when, and under what consent authorization.

## Warm Handoff Protocol

When referring a survivor to another agency, a "warm handoff" — where you directly connect
the survivor to the new provider rather than just giving them a phone number — dramatically
improves follow-through.

### Step-by-Step Warm Handoff

| Step | Action | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 1 | **Discuss the referral with the survivor.** Explain why you are recommending this agency, what services they provide, and what the survivor can expect. | Never refer without explaining. Survivors may distrust new agencies. |
| 2 | **Get consent.** Obtain written consent to share relevant information with the receiving agency. | Use your agency's consent form. Be specific about what will be shared. |
| 3 | **Call ahead.** Contact the receiving agency to confirm availability, explain the situation (with consent), and schedule an intake appointment. | Calling with the survivor present (on speaker) is ideal. |
| 4 | **Introduce by phone or in person.** If possible, do a three-way call or an in-person introduction so the survivor hears a familiar voice and a new one together. | This reduces the barrier of calling a stranger. |
| 5 | **Provide written information.** Give the survivor the agency name, contact person's name, appointment date/time, address, and what to bring. | Write it down — survivors under stress may not retain verbal info. |
| 6 | **Follow up.** Within 48 hours, check in with the survivor: Did they make it to the appointment? How did it go? Do they need anything else? | The handoff is not complete until the survivor is connected. |

## Coordinated Community Response (CCR) Framework

Beyond individual case coordination, advocates play a key role in the broader
**Coordinated Community Response** — the system-level approach to DV that connects all
institutions in a community.

### CCR Components and the Advocate's Role

| CCR Component | What It Is | Advocate's Role |
|---------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Criminal justice response** | Police, prosecutors, courts working together on offender accountability | Advocate for survivor-centered prosecution; identify gaps in law enforcement response; participate in ride-alongs or policy review |
| **Civil legal response** | Protective orders, custody, divorce, housing | Connect survivors to legal aid; accompany to court; provide documentation support |
| **Batterer intervention** | Court-ordered or voluntary programs for people who use violence | Understand what BIPs do and don't do; do NOT provide couples counseling referrals for DV; monitor compliance when court-ordered |
| **Child welfare** | CPS investigations, child safety | Advocate for the non-offending parent; ensure CPS understands DV dynamics; prevent children from being removed solely because the parent is a DV victim |
| **Healthcare** | Screening, SANE exams, documentation, referrals | Train healthcare providers on universal DV screening; ensure SANE exam availability; build referral pathways from ERs to DV services |
| **Faith communities** | Support, safety, accountability within religious institutions | Train clergy on DV dynamics; challenge harmful theological justifications for abuse; create safe disclosure pathways in congregations |
| **Schools** | Identification, reporting, support for children affected by DV | Train school staff on recognizing signs; establish referral pathways from schools to DV services; support school-based safety planning |
| **Housing** | Emergency shelter, transitional housing, permanent housing | Advocate for housing-first approaches; connect to HUD-funded DV housing programs; address lease issues and fair housing rights |
| **Community education** | Prevention, awareness, bystander intervention | Conduct community education; train employers on workplace DV policies; support prevention programming |
