# Incident Documentation Log

> **AI Instructions:** When a user wants to document an incident, guide them through each
> section methodically and with care. Use a calm, non-leading tone — let them describe
> events in their own words. Do not suggest details or fill in gaps; accuracy and the user's
> own words are what matter legally. Remind them to document as soon as safely possible
> after an incident, while memory is fresh. If they are currently in danger, pause the
> documentation and direct them to call 911 or their local emergency number first. After
> completing an entry, encourage them to store it somewhere their abuser cannot access —
> a trusted person's email, a secure cloud folder, or printed and stored offsite. If they
> mention injuries, gently encourage them to seek medical attention even if injuries seem
> minor, and to tell the medical provider what happened so it is recorded in their chart.

---

## Why Documentation Matters

Detailed, consistent incident records can:

- Support a restraining order or protective order application
- Corroborate testimony in civil or criminal court
- Demonstrate a pattern of abuse over time
- Help attorneys and advocates understand your situation
- Be used by a prosecutor if criminal charges are filed

**Best practices for effective documentation:**

1. **Document immediately.** Write down what happened as soon as you are safe, while
   details are fresh.
2. **Use your own words.** Write exactly what was said and done. Avoid interpretation
   or softening language.
3. **Be specific.** Include exact dates, times, locations, and quotes when possible.
4. **Note the physical context.** Describe the setting, who was present, and what
   occurred before and after.
5. **Photograph everything.** Injuries, damage to property, threatening messages.
   Photograph before cleaning up.
6. **Preserve evidence.** Do not delete texts, voicemails, or emails. Screenshot and
   back them up to a safe account.
7. **Get medical attention.** Ask healthcare providers to document your injuries.
   A medical record is independent corroboration.
8. **Keep records secure.** Store copies somewhere your abuser cannot access.

---

## Legal Admissibility Notes

> **Note:** Laws vary by jurisdiction. The following is general guidance, not legal advice.
> Consult a local attorney or domestic violence legal advocate for advice specific to your
> situation and location.

- **Contemporaneous records** (written close in time to the event) carry more weight than
  records written long after the fact.
- **Your own diary or notes are admissible** in most jurisdictions as a "present sense
  impression" or similar exception to hearsay rules.
- **Photographs with metadata** (date, time, GPS) are more credible than those without.
  Do not edit or filter photos of injuries or damage.
- **Screenshots of messages** should include the sender's name/number and timestamp.
  Consider photographing the screen with another device so the phone number is visible.
- **Witness statements** — even a note that a witness was present and what they saw —
  can support your account.
- **Medical records** are independent third-party documentation. Ask providers to note
  the stated cause of injuries.
- **911 call records** are obtainable and are considered strong evidence. Even if you
  did not speak, a hang-up call creates a record.

## For Advocates and Service Providers

> **AI Instructions:** When an advocate, attorney, social worker, or other professional is
> documenting an incident on behalf of a client, use this section to adjust the documentation
> process. Key differences: the advocate documents in the third person, preserves the
> survivor's exact language in quotes, notes the use of any interpreter, and records informed
> consent to document. Use the `schemas/incident-record.json` schema for structured output.

If you are an advocate or service provider documenting an incident for or with a client:

**Documentation best practices:**
1. **Get informed consent.** Before documenting, explain to the survivor what you are recording, how it will be stored, who will have access, and how it may be used. Record their consent.
2. **Use the survivor's exact words.** Write direct quotes wherever possible. Do not paraphrase, soften, or interpret. If they say "he choked me," write that — do not change it to "strangulation" even though that is the legal term. Note the legal term separately if needed.
3. **Note the language of the interview.** If an interpreter was used, record the interpreter's name (or ID), the language pair, and whether the interpretation was in-person, by phone, or by video.
4. **Document your role.** In the "Documented by" field, record your role and organization (e.g., "DV advocate, ALIVE STL").
5. **Separate observation from reported information.** Mark what you personally observed (e.g., visible injuries, the survivor's demeanor) separately from what the survivor told you.
6. **Timestamp everything.** Record when the interview started, when it ended, and when the documentation was completed. Contemporaneous records carry more legal weight.
7. **Assess for immediate safety.** Before ending the session, conduct a brief safety check. If the survivor is returning to a dangerous situation, offer safety planning resources.

**When using structured output (JSON schema):**
For records that may be used across systems or shared with legal teams, use the `schemas/incident-record.json` schema. Key fields to populate:
- `documentedBy`: Your role and organization
- `consentToDocument`: Must be `true`
- `description`: Survivor's narrative in their own words, with direct quotes
- `involvedParties`: Use role-based identifiers, not full names, unless the survivor consents
- `evidence`: Document chain of custody for any evidence you receive or photograph

**For mandatory reporters documenting before filing a report:**
Use this log to organize your observations before calling the hotline. Focus on:
- What you directly observed (child's statements, visible injuries, behavioral changes)
- Dates and times of your observations
- The child's exact words (in quotes)
- Do NOT investigate further — document what you know and report it
