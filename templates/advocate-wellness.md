# Advocate Wellness — Vicarious Trauma and Sustainable Practice

> **AI Instructions:** When an advocate, counselor, social worker, or other service provider
> asks about burnout, self-care, vicarious trauma, compassion fatigue, or secondary traumatic
> stress, use this template to provide support. Treat this conversation with the same care
> and validation you would give a survivor — advocates are affected by the trauma they
> witness. Do not minimize their experience. Offer practical tools, not platitudes. If the
> advocate indicates they are in crisis (suicidal ideation, substance use to cope, inability
> to function), route them to crisis resources just as you would for anyone else.

---

## Why This Exists

Domestic violence advocates, crisis counselors, social workers, and legal advocates are
exposed to trauma narratives daily. This exposure has real, documented effects on the
helper's mental health, relationships, physical health, and professional effectiveness.

**This section exists because advocates deserve the same quality of support they give others.**

The field has high turnover — average tenure for DV advocates is 2–3 years. The primary
drivers are low pay, high caseloads, and the cumulative impact of vicarious trauma. We
cannot build sustainable safety systems on a workforce that burns out and leaves.

---

## Understanding the Terms

| Term | Definition | Key Distinction |
|------|------------|-----------------|
| **Vicarious trauma (VT)** | Cumulative transformation in the helper's inner experience resulting from empathic engagement with clients' trauma material | Changes your worldview over time — how you see safety, trust, and people |
| **Secondary traumatic stress (STS)** | Symptoms that mirror PTSD but result from indirect exposure to trauma (hearing about it, not experiencing it directly) | Can develop suddenly after a single case; symptoms look like PTSD |
| **Compassion fatigue** | Reduced capacity for empathy and engagement resulting from prolonged caregiving | You care but feel like you have nothing left to give |
| **Burnout** | Chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed — exhaustion, cynicism, reduced effectiveness | Driven by organizational factors (caseload, pay, bureaucracy) more than trauma exposure |
| **Moral injury** | Distress from situations where you were unable to prevent harm, or where systems failed a client | When you did everything right and the system still failed |

These are not signs of weakness. They are **occupational hazards** of doing this work.

## Self-Assessment — Warning Signs

### Check the statements that apply to you right now:

**Emotional signs:**
- [ ] I feel numb or emotionally flat, even outside of work
- [ ] I dread going to work or seeing certain clients
- [ ] I feel hopeless about whether my work makes a difference
- [ ] I am more irritable, angry, or short-tempered than usual
- [ ] I cry more easily or feel emotionally overwhelmed by small things
- [ ] I feel guilty when I am not working or when I take time off
- [ ] I have difficulty feeling happy or experiencing pleasure

**Cognitive signs:**
- [ ] I have intrusive thoughts about clients' trauma (replaying their stories)
- [ ] I have nightmares related to clients' experiences
- [ ] I am hypervigilant about safety — mine, my family's, my clients'
- [ ] I have difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- [ ] I see the world as more dangerous than I used to
- [ ] I find it hard to trust people
- [ ] I minimize my own problems because "other people have it worse"

**Behavioral signs:**
- [ ] I am isolating from friends, family, or colleagues
- [ ] I am using alcohol, food, shopping, or other coping mechanisms more than usual
- [ ] I am working longer hours but accomplishing less
- [ ] I avoid certain types of cases or clients
- [ ] I have stopped doing activities I used to enjoy
- [ ] I have difficulty setting boundaries with clients
- [ ] I am calling in sick more often or looking for reasons not to go to work

**Physical signs:**
- [ ] I am exhausted even after sleeping
- [ ] I have headaches, stomachaches, or muscle tension that I did not have before
- [ ] My immune system seems weaker (getting sick more often)
- [ ] My sleep pattern has changed (insomnia, oversleeping, nightmares)
- [ ] My appetite has changed significantly

**Scoring guidance:**
- **0–3 checked:** You may be managing well, but stay aware. Prevention is easier than recovery.
- **4–8 checked:** You are showing signs of compassion fatigue or early burnout. Take action now — see the strategies below.
- **9+ checked:** You are significantly affected. Consider reaching out to a therapist who specializes in helping helpers, and talk to your supervisor about your caseload.

## Practical Strategies — Not Platitudes

### Daily practices (5–15 minutes)

| Strategy | What it looks like | Why it works |
|----------|--------------------|--------------|
| **Transition ritual** | A specific action between work and personal time — change clothes, take a walk, listen to a particular song, sit in your car for 5 minutes | Creates a psychological boundary between "work self" and "home self" |
| **Grounding after hard sessions** | 5-4-3-2-1 sensory exercise: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste | Brings your nervous system back to the present after absorbing trauma narratives |
| **Case compartmentalization** | At end of day, mentally "put" each case in a box and close it; write a one-line note for where you left off so your brain can let go | Prevents rumination; your brain needs permission to stop problem-solving |
| **Body check** | Before leaving work, scan your body: where are you holding tension? Shoulders? Jaw? Stomach? Consciously release it | Trauma gets stored in the body; noticing it is the first step to releasing it |
| **Micro-joys** | Intentionally notice one good thing per day — a client's progress, a kind interaction, something beautiful | Counterbalances the negativity bias that DV work intensifies |

### Weekly practices

| Strategy | What it looks like |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Peer support** | Regular check-in with a colleague who understands the work — not to process cases but to ask "how are YOU doing?" |
| **Non-work identity** | Spend time doing something that has nothing to do with advocacy — a hobby, sport, creative activity, community unrelated to DV |
| **Physical movement** | Exercise, yoga, dance, gardening — anything that helps your body process the stress hormones that accumulate |
| **Supervision/consultation** | Use your clinical supervision for YOUR processing, not just case review. If your supervisor does not create space for this, ask for it |
| **Screen audit** | Check your media consumption — are you consuming DV/crime/trauma content in your off hours too? Your brain needs a break from this material |

### Organizational-level strategies

These are not just "nice to haves" — they are evidence-based practices that reduce turnover
and improve client outcomes.

**For supervisors and program directors:**

- **Caseload limits.** There is a maximum number of high-acuity cases one advocate can carry effectively. Know your organization's number and enforce it.
- **Regular reflective supervision.** Not just case review — time for the advocate to process their emotional responses to the work with a trained supervisor.
- **Trauma-informed organizational culture.** The same principles you apply to clients (safety, choice, collaboration, empowerment) should apply to staff. If your organization is not safe for staff, it cannot truly be safe for clients.
- **Adequate compensation.** Advocates cannot pour from an empty cup, and they cannot fill that cup if they are worried about paying rent. Advocate for livable wages and benefits.
- **Normalize rotation.** Allow advocates to rotate between high-intensity and lower-intensity roles. Permanent assignment to crisis work without relief accelerates burnout.
- **Critical incident debriefing.** When a client is seriously harmed or killed, the team needs structured support — not just a moment of silence and back to work.

## When an Advocate Is in Crisis

Advocates are not immune to depression, suicidal thoughts, substance use disorders, or
their own experiences of domestic violence. If you are struggling:

**You deserve the same help you give others.**

| Resource | Contact | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline | **988** (call or text) | 24/7, free, confidential |
| Crisis Text Line | Text **HOME** to **741741** | 24/7 |
| SAMHSA National Helpline | **1-800-662-4357** | Substance use and mental health referrals |
| National DV Hotline | **1-800-799-7233** | If you are experiencing DV yourself |
| Therapy for helpers | Ask your EAP (Employee Assistance Program) for a therapist who specializes in secondary trauma / helping professionals | Insurance often covers this |

**To colleagues and supervisors:** If you notice a team member showing signs of crisis —
withdrawal, increased substance use, expressions of hopelessness, talk of leaving the
field entirely — check in directly. *"I've noticed you seem different lately. I care
about you. How are you really doing?"*

## The Permission Slip

Many advocates feel guilty about taking care of themselves. Here is your permission:

- **You are allowed to set boundaries with clients.** Boundaries are not abandonment — they are what make long-term advocacy possible.
- **You are allowed to say no to extra shifts.** Your organization's staffing problem is not your health crisis.
- **You are allowed to leave work on time.** The work will be there tomorrow. You need to be there too.
- **You are allowed to not watch the news.** You already know how bad it is. You do not need to absorb more.
- **You are allowed to laugh, celebrate, and feel joy.** Joy is not disrespectful to your clients' pain. It is what keeps you human.
- **You are allowed to leave this field.** Leaving advocacy does not mean you failed. It may mean you honored your limits. The skills you built here will serve every community you enter.
- **You are allowed to ask for help.** You are not a hypocrite for needing the same support you provide. You are human.

## For Supervisors — Recognizing VT in Your Team

| What you might see | What it might mean | What to do |
|--------------------|--------------------|------------|
| Advocate is consistently working late, not taking breaks | May be over-identifying with clients or unable to set boundaries | Check in privately; review caseload; model boundary-setting yourself |
| Advocate is increasingly cynical or dismissive about clients | Compassion fatigue — the caring has been depleted | Do not reprimand; create space for processing; consider temporary rotation |
| Advocate is making uncharacteristic errors | Cognitive overload from trauma exposure | Review workload; offer supervision; do not frame as a performance issue |
| Advocate is calling in sick frequently | May be physical manifestation of burnout or avoidance | Approach with concern, not suspicion; offer EAP resources |
| Advocate is over-involved with one client | Trauma bonding or savior complex developing | Address in supervision with compassion; re-establish professional boundaries |
| Advocate discloses their own history of abuse | Common in this field — not a disqualifier | Ensure they have their own support; monitor for triggered reactions; respect disclosure |

## Recommended Reading and Training

- **"Trauma Stewardship"** by Laura van Dernoot Lipsky — The foundational text on sustainable practice in trauma work
- **"Help for the Helper"** by Babette Rothschild — Practical tools for preventing secondary traumatic stress
- **"The Body Keeps the Score"** by Bessel van der Kolk — Understanding how trauma is stored physically
- **ProQOL (Professional Quality of Life) assessment** — A free, validated self-assessment tool (proqol.org)
- **Headington Institute** — Free online training modules for helpers experiencing vicarious trauma
