# Court Preparation Toolkit — Advocate Guide

> **AI Instructions:** When an advocate asks for help preparing a client for court — whether
> for a protective order hearing, custody hearing, criminal case, or any other proceeding
> related to domestic violence or abuse — walk them through the relevant sections of this
> toolkit. Adjust based on the type of hearing. Always remind the advocate that this is
> general guidance, not legal advice, and that a licensed attorney should review case-specific
> strategy. If the survivor does not have an attorney, refer to Pod 4 (Legal Protection)
> for legal aid resources.

---

## Purpose

Advocates accompany survivors to court every day. This toolkit provides structured guidance
for the three phases of court involvement: **before** (preparation), **during** (day-of
support), and **after** (follow-up). It covers the most common proceedings advocates
encounter in DV cases.

---

## Part 1 — Protective Order Hearings

### Before the Hearing

**Timeline checklist (work backwards from the hearing date):**

| Timeframe | Task | Done |
|-----------|------|------|
| **1–2 weeks before** | Review the petition with the survivor — ensure all incidents are documented | |
| **1–2 weeks before** | Identify and prepare witnesses (if any) | |
| **1–2 weeks before** | Gather supporting evidence (photos, screenshots, medical records, police reports) | |
| **1 week before** | Organize evidence in chronological order; make 3 copies (court, respondent's attorney, survivor's copy) | |
| **1 week before** | If survivor has an attorney, confirm pre-hearing meeting is scheduled | |
| **3–5 days before** | Do a courthouse walkthrough if the survivor has never been (or use the Courthouse Orientation below) | |
| **3–5 days before** | Plan logistics: transportation, childcare, time off work, parking | |
| **3–5 days before** | Discuss what to wear (clean, neat, conservative — no message clothing) | |
| **1 day before** | Review the survivor's statement; practice saying key facts out loud | |
| **1 day before** | Pack the court bag (see Court Day Bag Checklist below) | |
| **1 day before** | Confirm safe transportation to and from the courthouse | |
| **Morning of** | Safety check — is the abuser likely to be at the courthouse? Plan accordingly | |

**Preparing the survivor for testimony:**

> **Important:** Advocates do NOT coach testimony. The survivor tells the truth in their
> own words. The advocate's role is to help them feel prepared and reduce anxiety.

- **Practice speaking about the incidents out loud.** Many survivors have never said these things aloud to a stranger. Hearing their own voice say the words reduces the shock of doing it in court.
- **Explain the format.** "The judge will ask you questions, or your attorney will. Then the other side gets to ask questions. That part can feel aggressive, but the judge is there to keep it fair."
- **Prepare for common questions:**
  - What happened? (Be specific — dates, times, what was said and done)
  - Why are you afraid? (Connect specific behaviors to specific fears)
  - What do you want the court to order? (Distance, no contact, custody provisions, etc.)
  - Why didn't you report it sooner / call the police / leave? (The survivor should not have to justify this, but they should be prepared for the question)
- **Practice staying calm under cross-examination:**
  - "If you don't know the answer, say 'I don't know.' That is always okay."
  - "If you don't understand the question, say 'Can you rephrase that?'"
  - "Take a breath before answering. There is no rush."
  - "Look at the judge when you answer, not at the respondent."
  - "If you get emotional, that is okay. The judge has seen it before. Take a moment."

### Courthouse Orientation — What to Expect

Share this with the survivor before their first court appearance:

**Getting there:**
- Arrive 30–60 minutes early
- There will be a metal detector and bag screening at the entrance — this is normal
- No weapons, no recording devices (phones may need to be silenced or turned off in the courtroom)

**Inside the courthouse:**
- Find the courtroom number on your notice/summons
- Check in with the clerk's office or the victim/witness assistance office if one exists
- The advocate can usually sit with the survivor in the gallery or at the table (rules vary by court — ask the clerk)
- The respondent will be in the same courtroom. If you have safety concerns, tell the bailiff or court security immediately
- Many courthouses have separate waiting areas for petitioners and respondents — ask

**In the courtroom:**
- Stand when the judge enters and exits
- Address the judge as "Your Honor"
- Speak clearly and loudly enough to be heard
- Do not interrupt — wait for your turn
- The judge may ask questions directly — answer honestly
- Proceedings may be recorded (audio or court reporter)

**The advocate's role in the courtroom:**
- Sit with or near the survivor (where permitted)
- Take notes on what happens (this is your record, not the court's)
- Do NOT speak unless addressed by the judge
- Do NOT react visibly to testimony (no head shaking, sighing, or whispering)
- Be a calm, steady presence — your composure helps the survivor stay regulated
- If the survivor needs a break, you can pass them a note or quietly ask the attorney to request one

### Court Day Bag Checklist

| Item | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Copies of the petition and any existing orders (3 sets) | For court, respondent's counsel, and survivor |
| Evidence organized chronologically | Photos, screenshots, medical records, police reports |
| A folder or binder to keep everything organized | |
| Photo ID for the survivor | Required to enter some courthouses |
| Pen and notepad | For notes during the hearing |
| Snacks and water | Hearings can take hours; courthouses may not have vending |
| Phone charger | |
| Comfort items | Tissues, a stress ball, a photo of children (motivation) |
| Cash for parking | Some courthouses have metered or paid lots only |
| Contact list | Attorney, advocate office, emergency contacts |
| Safety plan | In case the hearing does not go as hoped |
| Change of clothes for children | If children are present and waiting |

## Part 2 — Criminal Court (Victim/Witness Support)

When the abuser has been charged criminally, the survivor's role shifts from petitioner
to victim/witness. This changes the dynamics significantly.

### Key Differences from Protective Order Hearings

| Protective Order Hearing | Criminal Case |
|--------------------------|---------------|
| Survivor is the petitioner (they filed) | The state/prosecution filed; survivor is a witness |
| Survivor can drop the case (in most jurisdictions) | Survivor cannot drop the case — the prosecutor decides |
| Survivor may represent themselves or have an attorney | Survivor does not have their own attorney in the criminal case (the prosecutor represents the state, not the survivor) |
| Lower standard of proof (preponderance of evidence) | Higher standard (beyond a reasonable doubt) |
| Survivor controls the narrative | Prosecutor controls the narrative; survivor testifies when called |

### Advocate's Role in Criminal Proceedings

1. **Explain the process.** Many survivors do not understand that they cannot "drop charges." Explain that the prosecutor makes that decision and why.

2. **Connect to the victim/witness advocate at the prosecutor's office.** Most prosecutor offices have a victim/witness coordinator. This person is the survivor's point of contact for:
   - Hearing dates and schedule changes
   - Testimony preparation
   - Victim impact statements
   - Restitution
   - Notification of plea offers

3. **Prepare for the possibility of testifying.** The survivor may be called to testify at trial. Preparation is similar to protective order hearings, with additional considerations:
   - The defense attorney's job is to create doubt — their questions may feel hostile
   - The survivor should not take it personally
   - The prosecutor should do a pre-trial preparation session with the survivor

4. **Explain victim rights.** In Missouri (and most states), crime victims have rights including:
   - Right to be informed of all court dates
   - Right to be present at proceedings
   - Right to be heard at sentencing
   - Right to restitution
   - Right to be notified of the offender's release (VINE system)
   - Right to a speedy disposition

5. **Address the emotional complexity.** Many survivors have complicated feelings about criminal prosecution — they may want the violence to stop but not want their partner imprisoned. Validate all of these feelings without judgment.

## Part 3 — Custody and Family Court

Custody proceedings in DV cases are among the most complex and high-stakes situations
advocates encounter.

### Critical Issues for Advocates to Know

**Custody and DV intersection:**
- In Missouri, courts must consider DV as a factor in custody determinations (RSMo § 452.375)
- A history of DV creates a rebuttable presumption against unsupervised custody in many states
- Abusers frequently use custody proceedings to continue controlling the survivor — this is well-documented as "litigation abuse" or "systems abuse"

**Common abuser tactics in custody cases:**
- Filing for full custody as a control mechanism
- Claiming parental alienation to discredit the survivor
- Using court-ordered exchanges as opportunities for contact or intimidation
- Dragging out proceedings to drain financial resources
- Presenting as a model parent in court while being abusive at home

**How advocates can help:**
- Document every custody exchange (date, time, location, what happened, witnesses)
- Help the survivor keep a detailed parenting log (children's statements, behavioral changes after visits, any concerning disclosures)
- Connect to a family law attorney experienced in DV cases (not all family attorneys understand DV dynamics)
- Advocate for supervised visitation or safe exchange locations when appropriate
- Help the survivor understand that their safety and the children's safety are connected — courts are increasingly recognizing this

### Safe Exchange Planning

When court-ordered custody exchanges happen, safety planning is critical:

- **Use a supervised exchange center** if available in your area
- **Use a public location** with security cameras (police station lobby, fire station, library)
- **Bring a support person** as a witness
- **Document everything** — arrival time, departure time, children's demeanor, anything said
- **Have the court order specify exact exchange terms** — location, time, who can be present, what happens if someone is late
- **Keep communication in writing** (text or email only) to create a record

## Part 4 — After Court

### Regardless of Outcome

1. **Debrief with the survivor.** Ask how they are feeling. Validate that going to court was brave.
2. **Review next steps.** Whether the outcome was favorable or not, what happens next?
3. **Update the safety plan.** The court outcome changes the safety landscape:
   - If a protective order was granted → review the terms, distribute copies, plan for enforcement
   - If denied → discuss alternative safety measures, appeal options, and reapplication timing
   - If criminal charges resulted in release or acquittal → heightened safety planning
4. **Document the outcome.** Record what happened in the advocate's case notes for continuity.
5. **Schedule follow-up.** Do not let the survivor leave without a next contact point.

### If the Protective Order Was Granted

- Help the survivor **understand every term** of the order
- Make **multiple copies**: one for the survivor to carry at all times, one for home, copies for school/childcare/employer/neighbors
- **Register the order** with local law enforcement (in Missouri, the clerk should forward it, but confirm)
- Review **what constitutes a violation** and what to do if one occurs:
  1. Get to safety
  2. Call 911 and report the violation
  3. Document the violation (date, time, what happened)
  4. Contact the advocate
- **Set a calendar reminder** for the expiration date — begin renewal process early

### If the Protective Order Was Denied

- **Validate the survivor's experience.** A denial does not mean the abuse did not happen. Courts require specific legal standards that may not have been met on this attempt.
- **Assess immediate safety.** The abuser may feel emboldened. Update the safety plan.
- **Discuss options:**
  - Can the petition be refiled with additional evidence?
  - Is a different type of order available (e.g., a no-contact order through criminal proceedings)?
  - Are there other legal remedies (criminal charges, tort claims)?
- **Connect to legal aid** if the survivor was unrepresented — an attorney may improve the outcome on a second attempt
